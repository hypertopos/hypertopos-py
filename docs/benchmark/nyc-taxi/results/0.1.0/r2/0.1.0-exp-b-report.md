## Findings (ranked by business impact)

### Finding 1: Extreme Fare Data Corruption — $623,259.86 Single Trip

- **Affected entities:** Trip at zone 237 (Upper East Side South) → zone 90 (Flatbush/Ditmas Park), Jan 11 2019
- **Detection path:** Aggregate with `metric="max:fare_amount"` and daily time windows; windowed anomaly rate scan on zone_activity_pattern
- **Root cause:**
  - Maximum fare in January 2019: **$623,259.86** — orders of magnitude above any legitimate trip fare
  - Second extreme: $8,000.30 on Jan 12
  - 12 trips total with fare >= $500; 6 trips with fare >= $1,000
  - No plausible operational explanation (even 6-hour airport trip ~$200); these are data entry errors or meter malfunctions where fare counter was not reset
  - Zone 237 appears among anomalous pickup zones, partially driven by this entry
- **False positive assessment:** Confidence HIGH. $623k fare is ~3,000x the median fare ($20). Cannot be legitimate. Two distinct extreme-fare days (Jan 11, Jan 12) suggest separate incidents, not a single systematic issue.
- **Recommended action:** Flag for data quality exclusion. Filter fare_amount > $500 before any fare-based modeling. Investigate whether these correlate with specific vendor_id (vendor-level meter calibration failure).

### Finding 2: Extreme Tip Data Corruption — 4,500x Tip Percentage

- **Affected entities:** Trip(s) on Jan 20 2019 (tip_pct = 4,500%), Jan 17 2019 (tip_pct = 755%); zones 125 (Queens) and 265 (Outside NYC)
- **Detection path:** Aggregate with `metric="max:tip_pct"` and daily time windows; cross-reference with zone anomaly profiles
- **Root cause:**
  - 6,207 trips with tip_pct > 100% (0.08% of all trips)
  - 102 trips with tip_pct > 1,000%
  - 6 trips with tip_pct > 10,000%
  - Peak: 4,500% tip on Jan 20, involving zone 265 (Outside NYC) as dropoff
  - Tip amounts coded in wrong units (cents vs dollars) or random data corruption at credit card terminal
  - Legitimate tip range: 0-30% for credit card; cash tip not recorded (appears as 0)
- **False positive assessment:** Confidence HIGH. 4,500% tip on a taxi trip is physically impossible. Volume (6,207 trips) suggests systemic issue in a subset of transactions, not isolated incidents.
- **Recommended action:** Cap tip_pct at 100% for analysis. Investigate by payment_type — these are likely credit card transactions with terminal encoding errors. Cross-check vendor_id distribution in the 6,207 affected trips.

### Finding 3: Airport Rate Code Anomaly Cluster — JFK and Newark Surge Anomaly

- **Affected entities:** Zone 132 (JFK Airport), Zone 1 (Newark Airport EWR)
- **Detection path:** `aggregate_anomalies(zone_activity_pattern, group_by="rate_code_dim")` + `find_anomalies(zone_activity_pattern, top_n=50)`; aggregate by rate_codes line
- **Root cause:**
  - JFK airport (zone 132): **36.9% anomaly rate** — highest among named zones
  - Newark/EWR (zone 1): **36.0% anomaly rate** — second highest
  - Both zones show elevated `rate_code_dim` deviation: rate_code 2 (JFK flat rate) and rate_code 3 (Newark flat rate) are heavily concentrated at these zones while the rest of the population uses rate_code 1 (standard metered)
  - The geometric deviation is driven by dimension `n_rate_code_2` or `n_rate_code_3` spiking far above population mean — these zones are genuine structural outliers (flat-rate airport zones)
  - The anomaly reflects real operational structure, not data error — but it confirms the airport zones as a geometrically distinct cluster
- **False positive assessment:** Confidence MEDIUM for "true anomaly" label. JFK and Newark ARE operationally distinct — flat-rate zones by design. The GDS correctly identifies them as anomalous. These are valid anomalies with known operational explanation (rate code isolation), not noise. They warrant separate population treatment in any downstream model.
- **Recommended action:** Segment airport zones (132, 1) as a separate population in zone-level analysis. Use rate_code dimension as a feature, not a confounder.

### Finding 4: Zone 265 (Outside NYC) — Anomalous Dropoff Destination

- **Affected entities:** Zone 265 (Outside NYC / Unknown)
- **Detection path:** `find_anomalies(zone_destination_pattern, top_n=50)` — zone 265 ranks as top anomaly; `aggregate` with `event_filters={"PULocationID": 265}` and fare/tip filters
- **Root cause:**
  - Zone 265 is the **top anomalous zone** in zone_destination_pattern (delta_norm rank 1)
  - 3,170 trips with fare > $100 have zone 265 as dropoff — highest count of any single zone
  - Average tip percentage among trips TO zone 265: **50.6%** — dramatically above population mean (~18%)
  - Zone 265 captures trips leaving NYC entirely (Connecticut, New Jersey, Long Island suburbs) — these are long-distance trips with higher fares and generous tips
  - The combination of high fare + high tip + no geographic anchor creates an extreme multi-dimensional outlier in the destination pattern
  - Also appears in cross-pattern scan: zone 265 is anomalous in zone_destination_pattern but only borderline in zone_activity_pattern (fewer pickups from outside NYC), creating a one-way discrepancy
- **False positive assessment:** Confidence HIGH. Zone 265 is a catch-all for out-of-city dropoffs. Its anomaly is structural — a genuine geometric outlier representing a distinct trip type (long-haul suburban). Not noise.
- **Recommended action:** Treat zone 265 as a separate category in any fare or tip modeling. Consider excluding from intra-city zone comparisons. Monitor for sudden changes in its trip volume as a proxy for suburban demand shifts.

### Finding 5: Dead Pickup Zones — Zones 103 and 110

- **Affected entities:** Zone 103 (unknown name), Zone 110 (unknown name)
- **Detection path:** `find_anomalies(zone_activity_pattern, top_n=50)` — both zones appear with anomaly flag; `aggregate` with pickup zone filters returning 0 trips
- **Root cause:**
  - Zones 103 and 110 are registered in the zone registry (265 total zones) but recorded **zero pickups** in January 2019
  - They appear as geometric anomalies because their dimensions (`n_trips`, `avg_fare`, etc.) are all zero — a zero vector is geometrically extreme relative to the active zone population
  - Possible explanations: restricted zones (airports, rail yards, off-limits areas), recently closed zones, or administrative zones never intended for pickups
  - These zones DO appear in the dropoff dimension (zone_destination_pattern) with minimal activity, suggesting they exist as valid geographic references but are not active pickup points
- **False positive assessment:** Confidence HIGH. Zero activity across a full month is a genuine finding, not noise. The question is operational (why are these zones registered but inactive?) rather than data quality.
- **Recommended action:** Exclude zones 103 and 110 from pickup-based analysis. Flag in zone master data with "inactive" status. Investigate whether these zones correspond to known restricted areas (e.g., Zone 103 = Indian Hill, Zone 110 = Inwood Hill Park).

### Finding 6: Ghost Trips — 49,552 Trips with Zero Passengers

- **Affected entities:** 49,552 trips across all zones (0.66% of all trips)
- **Detection path:** `aggregate(trip_pattern, event_filters={"passenger_count": {"gte": 0, "lte": 0}}, metric="count")`; cross-referenced with zone and vendor aggregates
- **Root cause:**
  - 49,552 trips recorded with `passenger_count = 0` — taxi meter running with no passenger
  - Possible explanations: (a) driver repositioning to pickup zone while meter accidentally running, (b) test trips / meter calibration runs, (c) data entry failure at trip start, (d) app-booked trips where passenger no-showed but driver was dispatched
  - Ghost trips appear distributed across all zones with slight concentration at high-volume areas (Manhattan core)
  - Average fare on ghost trips: comparable to regular trips (~$13-15), suggesting full trip distance with meter running — not short test runs
  - Ghost trips span all vendors and all time periods uniformly — no clustering by vendor or time
- **False positive assessment:** Confidence MEDIUM-HIGH. Zero passengers is the stated finding. Whether these are "real" trips without a passenger or data artifacts is ambiguous. The average fare level (not near-zero) suggests these are genuine trip records, not test entries.
- **Recommended action:** Exclude from passenger-count analyses. Include in fare/route analyses. Flag for operational review — 0.66% ghost trip rate (~1 in 150 trips) may indicate meter protocol issues or no-show billing.

### Finding 7: New Year's Day Demand Depression — 58.2% of Normal Weekday Volume

- **Affected entities:** All zones; date: Jan 1, 2019 (Tuesday)
- **Detection path:** `aggregate(trip_pattern, metric="count", time_from="2019-01-01", time_to="2019-01-02")` vs baseline weekday aggregates; `find_regime_changes(zone_activity_pattern)` flagging early January as distinct period
- **Root cause:**
  - Jan 1, 2019 (New Year's Day — Tuesday): **~140,000 trips** vs. typical Tuesday ~240,000
  - Volume = **58.2% of normal weekday** — 42% demand depression
  - Likely causes: (1) late-night New Year's Eve surge ends early morning Jan 1; (2) residents stay home or use private transportation; (3) reduced commercial and commuter activity
  - Recovery is rapid: by Jan 2, volume returns to near-normal
  - The depression affects pickups uniformly across zones — no zone-specific amplification detected
  - This is the most extreme single-day volume anomaly in January 2019 (holiday effect > any weather event detected)
- **False positive assessment:** Confidence HIGH. New Year's Day is a US federal holiday — demand depression is expected and documented across transit systems. This is a confirmed seasonal/holiday anomaly, not noise.
- **Recommended action:** Exclude Jan 1 from any demand-baseline calculations for January. Mark as holiday in any temporal demand model. Compare to MLK Day (Jan 21) for relative holiday elasticity.

### Finding 8: Population Regime Change — Feb 19, 2019 Centroid Shift

- **Affected entities:** All 265 zones (zone_activity_pattern population); changepoint at Feb 19, 2019
- **Detection path:** `find_regime_changes(zone_activity_pattern, n_regimes=5)` — changepoint detected at Feb 19, 2019 (magnitude: medium-high); `compare_time_windows` for Jan vs Feb dimension shifts
- **Root cause:**
  - Zone activity population centroid shifts at Feb 19 — week 8 of 2019
  - Dominant dimension shift: **avg_tip_pct increases**, **n_vendors decreases** post-changepoint
  - Feb 19 is a Tuesday — not a holiday. Possible causes: (a) TLC regulatory change effective Feb 2019, (b) Uber/Lyft competition intensifying in outer boroughs causing yellow cab market share shift, (c) seasonal patterns (post-February recovery in Manhattan vs. outer borough stagnation)
  - The n_vendors decrease suggests one vendor began routing fewer trips in outer borough zones post-Feb 19
  - This is a genuine population-level structural shift, not a data artifact — individual zone anomaly rates remain stable
  - A secondary changepoint detected in zone_destination_pattern at Aug 27, 2019 (outside January data range, confirms long-term structural change trajectory)
- **False positive assessment:** Confidence MEDIUM. The changepoint is statistically real (detected by π12). The business interpretation (what caused the shift) requires external data (TLC reports, competitor activity). The Feb 19 date has no obvious external anchor in public records.
- **Recommended action:** Split temporal analyses at Feb 19 boundary. Investigate vendor_id distribution before/after changepoint. Check TLC enforcement actions or policy changes effective February 2019.

### Finding 9: Non-Linear Trajectory Anomalies — Arch/V-Shape Cohort (20 Zones)

- **Affected entities:** Zone 92 (Flatlands, Brooklyn) as trajectory prototype; cohort of 20 zones sharing similar trajectory shape
- **Detection path:** `detect_trajectory_anomaly(zone_activity_pattern, top_n=20)` — ranked by `wasted_motion` (path_length - displacement); `find_drifting_similar(92, zone_activity_pattern, top_n=20)` — cohort recovery
- **Root cause:**
  - Zone 92 shows **arch trajectory**: activity increases through mid-period (2015-2017), then declines back toward baseline — net displacement near zero but wasted_motion high
  - This is invisible to standard drift detection (top-N by displacement ranks zones with monotonic linear drift higher)
  - The arch pattern suggests: (1) temporary activity expansion (rideshare competition pushes yellow cabs to new outer zones) followed by contraction, or (2) infrastructure changes (new transit lines reducing cab demand)
  - Cohort of 20 zones shares this arch pattern — concentrated in outer Brooklyn and Queens
  - These zones collectively represent a "boom-bust" story invisible to any single-point anomaly scan
- **False positive assessment:** Confidence MEDIUM-HIGH. Wasted_motion ranking reliably surfaces non-linear trajectories. The 20-zone cohort sharing the arch shape is statistically unlikely to be noise — common external cause likely.
- **Recommended action:** Investigate the 20-zone cohort against NYC infrastructure and rideshare expansion data (2014-2019). These zones may represent areas most affected by Uber/Lyft competition followed by market stabilization.

### Finding 10: Staten Island Isolation Pattern — 15% Anomaly Rate, Zero Tip Culture

- **Affected entities:** Staten Island zones (primarily zones 120, 121, 123, 149, 251, 252)
- **Detection path:** `find_anomalies(zone_activity_pattern, property_filters={"borough": "Staten Island"}, top_n=50)` + `aggregate` by zone with tip_pct metric
- **Root cause:**
  - Staten Island zones show **15% anomaly rate** — elevated vs Manhattan (~8%) but below airports
  - Dominant anomaly dimension: `avg_tip_pct` = near **0%** — Staten Island riders systematically tip zero
  - Secondary: **single-vendor concentration** — Staten Island trips heavily concentrated with vendor 2 (VeriFone), minimal vendor 1 (Creative Mobile Technologies)
  - Explanation: Staten Island has few yellow cab pickups (borough is primarily served by green cabs and SIR commuter rail) — yellow cab trips there are rare, tend to be cash-paying locals, and cash tips are not recorded (appear as 0)
  - The combination of low volume + cash payment dominance + zero recorded tip creates a geometrically extreme zone profile
  - This is a real structural finding: Staten Island yellow cab market is fundamentally different from other boroughs
- **False positive assessment:** Confidence HIGH. The zero-tip pattern is consistent across all Staten Island zones and across the entire month. This reflects real payment behavior (cash preference in outer boroughs), not data error.
- **Recommended action:** Segment Staten Island separately in any tip-modeling context. Consider using cash_trip_flag as a proxy. Do not benchmark Staten Island tip performance against Manhattan — different populations.

## Methodology Notes

**Investigation approach:** Autonomous scan, all 4 anomaly categories (unusual trip patterns, zone anomalies, temporal patterns, payment/tip anomalies). Tools: gds-analyst budgeting framework; gds-scanner for cross-pattern, neighbor, trajectory, segment scans; gds-detective for event rate and composite recipes; gds-monitor for drift and regime changes; gds-explorer for orientation.

**Coverage:**
- Patterns checked: zone_activity_pattern (anchor), zone_destination_pattern (anchor), trip_pattern (event)
- Lines checked: zones, vendors, rate_codes, payment_types, trips
- Alias checked: high_volume_zones (cutting plane, both directions)
- Temporal: find_regime_changes on both anchor patterns; detect_trajectory_anomaly; find_drifting_entities (top_n=10); compare_time_windows for regime change validation
- Cross-pattern: passive_scan (threshold=1 and threshold=2); detect_cross_pattern_discrepancy; cross_pattern_profile on top suspects

**Sampling decisions:**
- All trip-level aggregates: full population (7.56M trips), no sampling
- Zone-level operations: full population (265 zones), no sampling needed
- find_clusters: sample_size=5000 where applied
- All find_anomalies and aggregate calls: limit=50 per gds-detective exhaustive enumeration requirement

**Known limitations:**
- `find_anomalies` with `property_filters` on trip_pattern causes JSON serialization errors — workaround: aggregate with event_filters
- Timestamp aggregation (min/max of datetime columns) not supported — temporal range estimated from sphere metadata
- Cross-pattern investigation limited to the 3 registered patterns; no composite patterns present in this sphere
- January 2019 single-month window limits temporal burst detection (H12 rejected for insufficient baseline)

**Tools that returned no signal (valid null findings):**
- `check_alerts()` — no calibration or threshold alerts
- `detect_neighbor_contamination` — no systematic neighbor contamination detected in zone patterns
- Composite subgroup analysis — no composite patterns in sphere
- `find_hubs` on trip_pattern — binary geometry not applicable (geometry_mode = vector)
