## 3. Hypotheses and Testing

### Hypothesis 1: penalty_interest_count is the primary discriminator between good and bad loans

**Test:** `get_line_profile(accounts_stress, penalty_interest_count, group_by=loan_status)`
**Result:**
- Status B mean = 8.26 (median=5), std=8.44, max=40
- Status D mean = 5.98 (median=4), std=6.58, max=38
- Status A mean = 0.26 (median=0), Status C mean = 0.03 (median=0)
- Cohen's d (B vs non-bad) ≈ 1.30 from contrast_populations

**Verdict: CONFIRMED.** Penalty interest count separates bad from good loans with very large effect size (d=1.30). Status B accounts incur 8x more penalty interest than normal accounts on average.

---

### Hypothesis 2: Bad loan accounts also show distinctly negative balance trends and lower minimum balances

**Test:** `get_line_profile(accounts_stress, min_balance / balance_trend, group_by=loan_status)`
**Result:**
- min_balance: Status B mean = -4,444 CZK, Status D mean = -4,598 CZK vs Status A/C positive means (+761/+830 CZK). Cohen's d = -1.56.
- balance_trend: Status B mean = -10,469 CZK/period, Status D mean = -7,934 CZK/period vs null-loan mean +9,735.

**Verdict: CONFIRMED.** Negative minimum balance (going into overdraft) is a strong secondary signal with d=-1.56. Both B and D status loans show systematically negative balance trends, confirming deteriorating financial health. The `loan_stress_pattern` correctly prioritizes `_d_min_balance` (40% contribution).

---

### Hypothesis 3: The account_stress_pattern population is contaminated by non-loan accounts, diluting precision

**Test:** `find_anomalies(account_stress_pattern, top_n=30)` — inspecting which entities appear in top anomalies
**Result:** Of top-30 anomalies by delta_norm, 14 are non-loan accounts (no `loan_status`). Top anomalies include 3374, 10656, 2260, 5343, 7520, 2305, 5429 — all no-loan accounts with high penalty_interest_count. A second archetype (positions 19-30) shows accounts anomalous for *very high* min_balance (positive extreme) — also all normal accounts.

**False positive assessment:** The 493 anomalies in `account_stress_pattern` include:
- ~200 no-loan accounts with high penalty interest (population contaminants — no ground truth label)
- ~50+ accounts anomalous for *high* min_balance (wealthy accounts — FP from a default-detection perspective)
- 65 confirmed bad loans (true positives)

Estimated precision against the loan-default task: 65 / 493 ≈ **13.2%**. The pattern was not designed as a default-only detector — it covers all 4,500 accounts regardless of loan status.

**Verdict: CONFIRMED.** Population contamination is the primary driver of low precision. Building a filtered pattern on loan_accounts only (682 entities) would dramatically improve precision.

---

### Hypothesis 4: The high-activity alias boundary reveals accounts transitioning to anomalous behavior

**Test:** `attract_boundary(high_activity_accounts, account_behavior_pattern, direction="out")` and `direction="in"`
**Result:**
- `direction="in"` (at risk of leaving): all `is_in_segment=true`, delta_norm range 1.8–5.0. 2 are anomalous (keys 68, 635).
- `direction="out"` (about to enter): none anomalous, delta_norm range 1.8–4.3. All represent accounts borderline for high-activity classification.
- Low_engagement alias boundary: entities clustered at signed_distance ≈ +0.11 (inside) or -0.24 (outside), no bad loans in boundary zone.

**Verdict: PARTIALLY CONFIRMED.** The high-activity boundary contains 2 anomalous accounts (68, 635) in the "at risk of leaving" zone, but neither is a known bad loan. The alias boundaries reveal behavioral transitions but are not predictive of loan default in this sphere configuration.

---

### Hypothesis 5: Population-level regime shift occurred in 1996-1997 affecting balance metrics

**Test:** `find_regime_changes(account_stress_pattern)` + `compare_time_windows(1993-1995 vs 1997-1999)`
**Result:**
- 3 regime changepoints detected: 1996-03-15 (magnitude 0.340), 1996-09-11 (0.292), 1997-03-10 (0.288)
- Top changed dimensions: `_d_mean_balance` (+0.25 diff) and `_d_amount_std` (+0.23 diff) at each changepoint
- Full window comparison 1993-1995 vs 1997-1999: centroid_shift = **2.09** (interpretation: "significant drift"), driven by mean_balance (+1.85) and amount_std (+0.96)
- The top-15 drifters in both `account_stress_pattern` and `loan_stress_pattern` ALL share identical `dimension_diffs` (amount_std +3.95, mean_balance +4.83) — this is systemic population growth, not individual entity drift

**Verdict: CONFIRMED.** A major population-level shift occurred in 1996-1997. The entire loan population grew wealthier on average (higher mean_balance, higher amount_std), meaning the normalization baseline shifted. This is consistent with Czech economic growth in that period. This systemic drift is not individual account anomaly — it affects the reference frame for all anomaly scores.

## 6. Temporal Analysis

### 6.1 Regime Changes in account_stress_pattern

Three changepoints detected in 1996-1997:
1. **1996-03-15** (magnitude 0.340): mean_balance shift +0.252, amount_std +0.228
2. **1996-09-11** (magnitude 0.292): mean_balance shift +0.258, amount_std +0.136
3. **1997-03-10** (magnitude 0.288): mean_balance shift +0.236, amount_std +0.166

All three shifts are in the same direction (mean_balance and amount_std increasing). `penalty_interest_count` showed zero shift across all changepoints — confirming that financial distress did not change at the population level during this period. Only the reference balance/activity level shifted.

**Business interpretation:** The Czech banking sector saw a growth phase in 1996-1997. The population as a whole became wealthier and more active (higher balances, larger transaction amounts). Stress indicators (penalty_interest_count) were unaffected — financially distressed accounts remained anomalous throughout.

### 6.2 Drift Analysis

Both `account_stress_pattern` and `loan_stress_pattern` top-15 drifters share **identical dimension_diffs**: amount_std +3.95/+4.58 and mean_balance +4.82/+5.24. This is a signature of systemic population drift, not individual entity drift. The drift ranking is dominated by accounts that grew significantly wealthier over the observation window (high amount_std + high mean_balance growth), masking genuine individual-level changes.

For loan_stress_pattern, account 4616 (loan_status=C, anomaly_tenure=19 slices) is the top drifter with reputation=0.89. This account shows high, chronic anomaly due to exceptional wealth growth — not a default risk.

### 6.3 Temporal Depth on Account 2335

Account 2335 (worst bad loan) has been anomalous since first observation (1992-12-31). Across all 25 temporal slices:
- delta_norm range: 16.31–16.70 (negligible variation)
- penalty_interest_count delta: constant at 15.13
- Regime changes at slice 13 (1996-03-15) caused small fluctuations in amount_std/mean_balance deltas only
- **Conclusion:** This account's financial distress predates the data window. The geometry captured a static, extreme condition — not a deteriorating trend.

## 9. False Positive Assessment

### 9.1 The Two FP Archetypes in account_stress_pattern

**Type A FP (majority): No-loan accounts with high penalty_interest_count**
- ~200 of 493 anomalies are accounts with no loan, flagged purely for high penalty interest count
- Example: account 3374 (no loan, penalty_interest_count=33, delta_norm=16.28) — 3rd highest anomaly in the pattern
- Business impact: These may represent legitimate over-limit fee situations, but without loan risk they are irrelevant to the default detection task
- Why it matters: This contaminates the anomaly list and lowers operational precision to ~13%

**Type B FP: High-balance (wealthy) accounts**
- ~50 of 493 anomalies are accounts driven by very high `_d_min_balance` (positive direction — very high floor balances)
- Example: account 378 (loan_status=C, min_balance=15,099 CZK, delta_norm=9.37, dim4 contribution 74%)
- Account 9640 (loan_status=A, min_balance=7,055 CZK, delta_norm=9.30) appears as anomalous but is a fully-paid loan
- Business impact: Wealthy accounts skew the population mean, causing them to appear geometrically extreme even when financially healthy
- Why it matters: A bank screening for defaults using this pattern would investigate wealthy healthy accounts — wasted investigative effort

### 9.2 Precision Improvement Path

Building a filtered `loan_accounts_stress_pattern` using only the 682 loan-holding accounts (the `loan_accounts` line) would:
1. Remove Type A FPs entirely (no-loan accounts excluded)
2. Significantly reduce Type B FPs (wealthy no-loan accounts excluded)
3. Expected precision improvement: from ~13% to potentially >50%
4. The `loan_stress_pattern` partially achieves this (recall 30.3%) but uses different dimensions

The combination of:
- Filter: loan-holding accounts only
- Dimensions: penalty_interest_count, min_balance, balance_volatility, income_coverage, balance_to_loan (as in loan_stress_pattern)
- would be the optimal single-pattern configuration
