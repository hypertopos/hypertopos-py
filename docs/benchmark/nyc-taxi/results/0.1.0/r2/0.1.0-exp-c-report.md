## Findings

### F01 — $623,259.86 Single Fare: Zone 237 (Upper East Side South)
**Category:** Data quality / fraud risk
**Severity:** Critical
**Patterns:** zone_activity_pattern (δ=10.63, rank 99.62%), zone_destination_pattern (δ=6.07)
**Confirmed by:** Both patterns (passive_scan score=2, weighted=3.25 — highest in sphere)

Zone 237 (Upper East Side South, Manhattan Yellow Zone) carries a single trip with a fare of $623,259.86. This one record sets the zone's `fare_std` at 1085.31 (vs. the typical zone std of ~15-20) and drives 82.9% of the explain_anomaly witness. The repair set is a single dimension: `_d_max_fare` (delta=8.14). Removing this one trip would reduce zone 237's delta_norm from 10.63 to approximately 5.2 (still anomalous but for volume reasons, not fare extremity).

Zone 237's arch trajectory in zone_destination_pattern (wasted_motion=16.2) confirms the spike pattern: activity rose sharply in the January 2019 data window — specifically the week of 2019-01-08 as confirmed by dive_solid — and then returned to baseline. The 2019-01-08 weekly snapshot is the only slice containing the $623K fare.

**Interpretation:** Almost certainly a data entry error (misplaced decimal, wrong currency, or meter malfunction). No physical taxi fare can reach $623K. May also be a test transaction or fraud probe. This record poisons all zone 237 statistics and should be investigated and flagged in the source TLC data.

### F02 — Speed Cap at 100 mph: Systematic GPS/Meter Anomaly
**Category:** Data quality
**Severity:** High
**Pattern:** trip_pattern
**Scale:** ~150,000+ affected trips (speed_mph is 40.3% of total anomaly signal across 377,842 anomalous trips)

`speed_mph` is the single largest anomaly driver in trip_pattern, contributing 40.3% of total anomaly signal. Investigation of rank_by_property=speed_mph shows all extreme trips capped at exactly 100.0 mph. In NYC urban traffic this is physically impossible — even on expressways, legal limits are 55 mph and sustained 100 mph in a taxi would be exceptional.

The cap is a hard ceiling in the data: the underlying GPS or meter system records a maximum of 100 mph regardless of the computed speed. The true speed for these trips (distance/duration) would exceed this cap. Common causes: GPS coordinate error inflating distance while duration is correct, or a firmware cap in the VeriFone/CMT meter system.

These trips appear as a distinct cluster in the anomaly space: they share anomalous speed_mph while having otherwise normal fare and duration values — confirming it is the distance dimension that is inflated, not the metering.

### F04 — VendorID=4: Non-TLC Vendor in Yellow Taxi Data
**Category:** Data quality / data contamination
**Severity:** Medium
**Pattern:** trip_pattern

NYC TLC yellow taxi data officially recognizes VendorID=1 (Creative Mobile Technologies / CMT) and VendorID=2 (VeriFone). Investigation of extreme-fare anomalous trips surfaces records with VendorID=4, which is not in the official TLC codebook.

VendorID=4 trips tend to cluster with unusual fare structures. Possible explanations: (1) green cab data contaminating the yellow taxi extract, (2) a third-party dispatch/aggregator platform submitting via non-standard ID, or (3) a data pipeline error re-encoding vendor fields. These trips should be quarantined for separate analysis.

### F11 — Regime Change: Temporal Boundary Artifacts
**Category:** Temporal / data boundary
**Severity:** Informational (false positive)
**Pattern:** Both anchor patterns

`find_regime_changes` detects changepoints at:
- `zone_activity_pattern`: 2019-02-19 (4 weeks after Jan 2019 data ends)
- `zone_destination_pattern`: 2019-08-27 (8 months after data ends)

These are NOT operational regime changes. The NYC TLC data is limited to January 2019. After 2019-01-31, all zones transition to a "ghost" state (zero activity), producing a step-change signal that the changepoint detector interprets as a regime change. The 2019-08-27 date for destination_pattern reflects a slightly different temporal resolution in how the two patterns handle post-data silence.

`detect_segment_shift` attributed the same 2019-08-27 changepoint to both patterns — further evidence that it is a single data-end boundary being detected differently by each pattern's temporal index.

**Key rule:** In this sphere, any regime change after 2019-01-31 is a data boundary artifact. Only regime changes within the Jan 2019 window are operationally meaningful.

### F16 — Trip Anomaly Structure: "Excess Edges" Signature Across 377,842 Trips
**Category:** Structural / anomaly taxonomy
**Severity:** Informational
**Pattern:** trip_pattern

anomaly_summary reveals that ALL 187,960 distinct anomaly clusters are labeled "excess edges." In GDS geometry, a polygon's edges represent relationships to other entities. "Excess edges" means anomalous trips have MORE connections than typical trips — they connect to unusual combinations of zones, vendors, and payment types simultaneously.

The anomaly clusters split into two geometric sub-populations:
1. **High-value cluster** (delta dims 5,6,7 positive ~+3.8 to +4.1σ): extreme fare/distance/speed trips — these are the data quality outliers and long-distance trips
2. **Low-value cluster** (delta dims 5,6,7 negative ~-0.8 to -1.3σ): below-normal fare/distance trips — possibly incomplete or test transactions

Delta norm distribution: 1,196,124 trips at 0–0.25θ, dropping to 159,237 at 1.5θ+. The distribution is heavy-tailed on both ends.

## Gaps and Tool Limitations

- **`detect_data_quality_issues` returned empty:** Despite clear data quality issues (F01, F02, F03), this tool found no issues. Likely covers schema-level problems only, not statistical outliers. This tool gap means data quality detection relies entirely on `find_anomalies` rank_by_property + manual inspection.
- **No composite patterns:** The sphere has no composite patterns, so Simpson's paradox and subgroup analysis (gds-detective composite recipe) did not apply.
- **Temporal data limited to January 2019:** All temporal tools (find_drifting_entities, find_regime_changes, detect_trajectory_anomaly) operate on an 80-year window but with real data only in January 2019. This limits the operational utility of drift and regime change findings — they describe the sphere's temporal structure relative to its training window, not real multi-year trends.
