# NYC Yellow Taxi — Anomaly Report
**Sphere:** `nyc_taxi_2019_jan` — NYC Yellow Taxi, January 2019
**Run:** Experiment A, r4
**Date:** 2026-03-31
**Tool:** hypertopos MCP 0.1.0
**Dataset:** 7,556,807 trips · 265 zones · 3 patterns

---

## Executive Summary

Navigation across all three patterns (trip_pattern, zone_activity_pattern, zone_destination_pattern) surfaced 15 distinct anomalies organized into three tiers:

- **Tier 1 — Data Quality:** Five trip-level recording errors with corrupted/impossible field values.
- **Tier 2 — Structural Zone Anomalies:** Ten zones with statistically extreme profiles, split between genuinely unusual geography (airports, ghost zones, outer Staten Island) and artifact zones driven by bad records.
- **Tier 3 — Temporal Drift and Regime Change:** One population-level regime shift and multiple zones with sustained behavioral drift across time.

The most severe single finding is a **$623,259.86 fare record** — a 12,000× multiple of the 99th percentile — that corrupts zone-level fare statistics for the entire upper Manhattan area.

## Tier 1: Data Quality Anomalies

### A1 — Extreme Fare Record ($623,259.86)

**Severity: Critical | Confidence: Very High**

| Stat | Value |
|------|-------|
| `fare_amount` max | $623,259.86 |
| `fare_amount` p99 | $52.00 |
| `fare_amount` median | $9.00 |
| Ratio max/p99 | ~12,000× |
| `fare_amount` mean | $12.24 (pulled from median $9.00 by outliers) |
| `fare_amount` std | $226.99 (18.5× mean) |

A single trip record contains a fare of $623,259.86. This is physically impossible — the standard NYC taxi fare is $2.50 initial charge plus $0.50 per 1/5 mile. At 831.8 miles (the dataset's max trip distance), the fare would be approximately $830. The $623K record is a data entry or system error.

**Why it matters:** This record propagates into zone-level aggregate statistics, making it appear that Upper East Side South (zone 237) has an extreme `fare_std` of $1,085 and a `max_fare` of $623K — flagging it as the #1 anomalous zone (delta_norm = 10.63) with severity=high. Lenox Hill West (zone 141) is similarly inflated. The zone anomaly scores are largely artifacts of this single bad row.

Without this record, zone 237 would still be a high-volume zone (+4.03 sigma trip_count) but would not be a severity=high anomaly.

### A2 — Impossible Tip Percentage (4,500%)

**Severity: Critical | Confidence: Very High**

| Stat | Value |
|------|-------|
| `tip_pct` max | 4,500.0 (= 4,500%) |
| `tip_pct` p99 | 0.389 (= 39%) |
| `tip_pct` median | 0.209 (= 21%) |
| `tip_pct` p25 | 0.0 (cash trips, tip not recorded) |
| Ratio max/p99 | ~11,571× |

A tip percentage of 4,500% is not a generous passenger — it is a data recording error. The most likely explanation is a unit mismatch (percentage entered as a decimal then re-multiplied), meter malfunction, or a direct entry error.

**Why it matters:** The overall population tip_pct mean (0.157) is dragged below the median (0.209) by the zero-tip cash trips, but the 4,500% record inflates the std significantly. The regime change on 2019-02-19 lists `_d_avg_tip_pct` as the top shifted dimension (+3.47 sigma) — some of that shift may be artifact from this record appearing in certain time windows.

### A3 — Impossible Trip Distance (831.8 miles)

**Severity: High | Confidence: Very High**

| Stat | Value |
|------|-------|
| `trip_distance` max | 831.8 miles |
| `trip_distance` p99 | 18.9 miles |
| `trip_distance` median | 1.55 miles |
| Ratio max/p99 | ~44× |

831.8 miles is the approximate road distance from New York City to Nashville, Tennessee. A yellow taxi cannot leave NYC jurisdiction. Even airport runs to JFK average 16.3 miles. This is a GPS or odometer recording error.

**Why it matters:** Any downstream analysis using distance (e.g., per-mile rate calculations, routing models, carbon footprint estimates) would be severely distorted if this record is included without cleaning.

### A4 — Speed Cap at 100.0 mph

**Severity: Medium | Confidence: High**

| Stat | Value |
|------|-------|
| `speed_mph` max | 100.0 (exactly) |
| `speed_mph` p99 | 37.2 |
| `speed_mph` median | 10.8 |
| `speed_mph` mean | 12.6 |

The maximum speed is an exact round number — 100.0 mph — which is almost certainly an artificial ceiling applied during data preprocessing rather than a real observed speed. NYC traffic averages 10–15 mph; no yellow taxi legally or practically reaches 100 mph. Some number of trips are capped at exactly this value, masking their true (erroneous or edge-case) speed computations.

**Why it matters:** Any speed-based anomaly detection downstream will miss records above the cap. The true distribution of extreme speeds is hidden.

**Severity: Low | Confidence: High**

`fare_amount` minimum = $0.01. The regulatory minimum fare in NYC is $2.50. A one-cent fare is either a test record, a meter calibration run, or a system default for cancelled/no-charge trips. These records contribute to fare averages and could distort per-trip metrics.

## Tier 2: Zone-Level Structural Anomalies

### B1 — Ghost Zones: Governor's Island (103) and Great Kills Park (110)

**Severity: Low | Confidence: Very High**

| Zone | Name | Borough | trip_count | delta_norm |
|------|------|---------|-----------|-----------|
| 103 | Governor's Island/Ellis Island/Liberty Island | Manhattan | **0** | 5.44 |
| 110 | Great Kills Park | Staten Island | **0** | 5.44 |

Both zones have zero trips, zero fare, zero vendors in January 2019. Governor's Island is accessible only by ferry; Great Kills Park is a waterfront nature preserve. Yellow taxis do not and cannot operate there.

These are **registered zone IDs that generate no taxi activity** — they exist in the zone taxonomy but not in the operational world. Their anomaly is driven entirely by `_d_n_vendors` = −4.09 sigma (no vendors at all) plus all other dimensions = 0.

**Why it matters:** These zones inflate the count of anomalous zones (they are borderline anomalies at ratio=1.02, just above the theta threshold of 5.35). Any model treating anomaly count as a signal should exclude known-inactive zones. They are structural, not operational anomalies.

**Severity: Medium | Confidence: High**

| Dimension | Value |

Zone 264 is an undefined location — nearly 150,000 trips in January 2019 had no valid pickup zone recorded. This is a **data completeness failure**: the zone assignment field is absent or invalid for 2% of trips. These trips are otherwise real (they have fares, distances, and tips), but their geographic origin is unknown.

Cross-pattern check: zone 264 is anomalous as a pickup zone but appears **normal as a dropoff zone**, suggesting the GPS failure or zone assignment problem occurs predominantly at trip start, not trip end.

### B3 — "Outside of NYC" Dropoffs (Zone 265)

**Severity: Medium | Confidence: High**

| Dimension | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| zone_name | Outside of NYC |
| borough | N/A |
| dropoff_count | 13,897 |
| dropoff_avg_fare | $70.77 |
| dropoff_avg_distance | 16.96 miles |
| dropoff_avg_tip | **0.506 (50.6%)** |
| delta_norm | 8.07 (99.6th percentile) |

13,897 trips (0.18%) terminated outside NYC boundaries. These are long-distance rides — likely to airports outside the city, suburban addresses, or New Jersey. The anomaly is driven by `_d_dropoff_avg_tip` = +7.23 sigma (50.6% average tip vs ~15% for Manhattan zones).

The high tip likely reflects that passengers on long out-of-city runs tend to be wealthier, tipping more generously for the extended service. The anomaly is **real and structurally explained** — these are a distinct trip category, not errors.

**Why it matters:** This zone provides a clean signal for out-of-city trip segmentation. Models treating all zones uniformly will misclassify these as fare outliers.

### B4 — JFK Airport (Zone 132): Revenue Concentration

**Severity: Medium | Confidence: High**

| Dimension | Value | Deviation |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| trip_count | 188,257 | +2.48 sigma |
| total_fare | **$8,791,742** | +4.31 sigma (highest of all zones) |
| avg_fare | $46.70 | +2.05 sigma |
| avg_distance | 16.27 miles | +2.59 sigma |
| fare_std | $16.30 | normal |
| delta_norm | 6.37 |

JFK generates the highest total fare revenue of any pickup zone despite having fewer trips than the busiest Manhattan zones. The anomaly is structural and legitimate: JFK-origin trips are uniformly long (16+ miles to Manhattan) with metered fares well above average.

**Cross-pattern discrepancy:** JFK is anomalous as a *pickup* zone but **normal as a dropoff zone**. This makes operational sense — passengers arriving at JFK take taxis to Manhattan (high-value pickup), but taxi drivers returning to JFK to drop off passengers do not earn the same return fare. There is an inherent asymmetry in airport taxi economics.

**Why it matters:** Revenue concentration at JFK is a real operational pattern. Any capacity planning, surge pricing, or driver incentive analysis must treat airport zones as structurally distinct from urban zones.

## Tier 3: Temporal Anomalies

### C1 — Population Regime Change on 2019-02-19

**Severity: Medium | Confidence: High**

A population-level regime change was detected with magnitude 4.63 (threshold 4.054) at `2019-02-19T23:00:00 UTC`. The top shifted dimensions in zone_activity_pattern:

| Dimension | Shift |
|-----------|-------|
| `_d_avg_tip_pct` | +3.47 sigma |
| `_d_n_vendors` | −1.99 sigma |
| `_d_fare_std` | −1.37 sigma |

Interpretation: After ~February 19, the average tip percentage rose sharply while the number of active vendors decreased and fare variability fell.

**Note on date range:** The sphere is labeled "January 2019" but this regime change occurs in late February. The temporal data appears to span beyond the nominal dataset period, suggesting the pattern calibration covers at least January–February 2019 or uses a rolling window. The February date may correspond to a specific NYC event — notably, February 2019 saw severe cold weather (the Polar Vortex affecting the Northeast) which could explain reduced vendor activity and changed passenger behavior. This should be cross-referenced with external event data.

## Recommended Actions

1. **Remove or flag the $623K fare record** (A1) before any aggregation. It corrupts zone-level max_fare and fare_std for all zones where it appears as a pickup.
2. **Investigate and cap extreme tip_pct** (A2). Records above, say, 5.0 (500%) are almost certainly errors.
3. **Remove or flag the 831.8-mile trip** (A3). Distances beyond ~50 miles are worth manual review for NYC yellow taxis.
4. **Document the speed_mph = 100.0 cap** (A4) as a known preprocessing artifact in any derived dataset.
5. **Exclude ghost zones 103 and 110** from zone-level analytics — they contribute noise to anomaly rate calculations.
6. **Investigate zone 264 ("N/A") data pipeline** (B2) — 2% of trips with missing pickup zone is a significant completeness gap.
7. **Treat airport zones (132, 138) as a separate tier** in any modeling that uses zone-average fare or distance — they are structurally different from urban zones.
8. **Cross-reference the 2019-02-19 regime change** with NYC operational events (weather, regulatory changes, vendor changes) to determine root cause.
