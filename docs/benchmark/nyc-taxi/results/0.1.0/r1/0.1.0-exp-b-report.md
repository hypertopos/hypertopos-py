### Finding 1: $623,259 Fare — Upper East Side South Fare-Spike Anomaly

**Affected entities:** Zone 237 (Upper East Side South, Manhattan)

**Detection path:** `profiling_alert` on `fare_amount` → `find_anomalies(zone_activity_pattern, rank_by_property=fare_std)` → zone 237 ranked #1 (fare_std = 1,085.3, delta_norm = 10.63, 99.6th percentile). `explain_anomaly` confirmed: severity=high, witness dim = `_d_max_fare` (delta=7.64, 51.7% contribution). `dive_solid` pinpointed the burst.

**Root cause:**
- `explain_anomaly`: The anomaly is driven by `_d_max_fare` (delta=7.64) with supporting elevation in `_d_total_fare` (+4.31) and `_d_trip_count` (+4.03). Conformal p=0.003774 (very significant).
- `dive_solid`: The spike is confined entirely to the week of **2019-01-08** (slice 523, delta_norm_snapshot=8.94). Before (2019-01-01, snapshot=2.71) and after (2019-01-15, snapshot=2.82) are normal. This is a one-week burst, not a sustained anomaly.
- The $623,259.86 fare was a single trip originating from zone 237 (Upper East Side South), confirmed by: 7 zones with fares >$500, zone 237 appearing as one of them, alongside zones 264 (N/A — unknown zone, 2 trips), 90, 141, 162, 226, 265.
- The fare_std for zone 237 across the full month is 1,085 vs. the p99 of 55.44 across all zones — a 19.6x ratio — entirely attributable to this single outlier trip.

**False positive assessment:** NOT a false positive. The $623K fare is a real data record in the TPEP system (both CMT and VeriFone recorded similar amounts: CMT=3 trips >$500, VeriFone=1 trip >$500 in January). However, it is almost certainly a data entry error or system glitch — NYC yellow taxi fares are metered and a legitimate $623K fare is physically impossible for a January intra-Manhattan trip. The fare_std of 1,085 ($1,085/trip average standard deviation) is a mathematical artifact of this single outlier.

**Confidence:** HIGH — 3 tools confirm (profiling_alert + explain_anomaly + dive_solid), root cause isolated to single week.

**Recommended action:** Flag trip records with fare_amount > $1,000 originating from standard-rate or negotiated-fare zones for human review. Implement fare plausibility caps in TPEP ingestion pipeline.

### Finding 2: Rate Code Misuse — JFK/Newark/Nassau Codes with Anomalous Trip Profiles

**Affected entities:** Rate code groups JFK (key=2), Newark (key=3), Nassau/Westchester (key=4), Negotiated fare (key=5)

**Detection path:** `aggregate(trip_pattern, group_by_line=rate_codes, geometry_filters={is_anomaly:true})` revealed catastrophic anomaly rates in non-standard rate codes.

**Root cause:**

| Rate Code | Total Trips | Anomalous Trips | Anomaly Rate | Avg Fare |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Standard (1) | 194,478 | 195,613 | **1.0%** | $11.13 |
| JFK (2) | 4,089 | 149,618 | **36.6%** | $52.00 |
| Negotiated (5) | 999 | 18,332 | **18.4%** | $43.58 |
| Newark (3) | 317 | 10,550 | **33.3%** | $67.63 |
| Nassau/Westchester (4) | 112 | 3,727 | **33.3%** | $70.63 |
| Unknown (99) | 5 | 2 | 0.4% | $9.95 |

Key observations:
- JFK flat fare should be $52 — confirmed ($52.00 avg). But 36.6% of JFK-coded trips are geometrically anomalous, suggesting many trips coded as JFK do not originate/terminate at JFK.
- Negotiated fare rate code (5) had 1,919 trips with fare >$100, second highest behind Nassau/Westchester (908 trips >$100). This code is frequently misused for high-fare trips that should carry a different code.
- Rate code 99 (undocumented) recorded 110 trips total (103 via fare-amount query), mostly cash-paid, with no standard code name — indicates system-generated or corrupted rate code entries.
- `aggregate(event_filters={RatecodeID>5})` found 110 trips using an undocumented rate code >5 (rate code 6 has only 7 trips, but 110 trips had RatecodeID=99 or other invalid values), predominantly paid in cash (92 cash vs. 16 credit).

**False positive assessment:** The geometric anomaly rates for JFK/Newark codes are real — these reflect structurally different trip profiles (long distance, high fare, airport-specific patterns) that are legitimately unusual compared to the standard-rate population mean. The signal is structural, not fraudulent, but the 36% rate warrants verification that JFK-coded trips actually connect to JFK Airport (zone 132).

**Confidence:** HIGH — aggregate counts confirmed, rate breakdowns consistent across two independent queries.

**Recommended action:** Audit trips with RatecodeID=2 that do NOT originate or terminate at zone 132 (JFK). Purge RatecodeID=99 records or map to correct codes. Build a JFK-specific pattern to separate airport trips from standard population.

### Finding 3: Ghost Zones — Permanently Zero-Activity Zones Flagged as Anomalous

**Affected entities:** Zone 103 (Governor's Island/Ellis Island/Liberty Island, Manhattan), Zone 110 (Great Kills Park, Staten Island)

**Detection path:** `find_anomalies(zone_activity_pattern)` → both zones appear with identical delta signatures (delta_norm=5.44, 95.1st percentile, tied). `dive_solid` confirmed: identical zero-state across all 18 temporal slices from 2008-12-30 through 2088-01-20. Zero trips, zero fare, zero vendors across the entire observation history.

**Root cause:**
- Both zones have `trip_count=0`, `total_fare=0`, `avg_fare=0`, `fare_std=0`, `max_fare=0`, `avg_distance=0`, `avg_tip_pct=0`, `n_vendors=0`.
- Primary anomaly driver: `_d_n_vendors` delta=-4.09 (56.4% contribution), meaning zero vendors where the population average expects multiple.
- The solid is completely flat at 5.4411 across all 18 snapshots — this is a permanent structural state, not a temporal anomaly.
- Governor's Island and Great Kills Park are inaccessible by yellow taxi by design (island/park geography). Their inclusion in the zone taxonomy as "Yellow Zone" and "Boro Zone" respectively is a data schema error.

**False positive assessment:** The geometric anomaly is technically correct — these zones ARE geometrically anomalous because they have zero activity. But the BUSINESS interpretation is not fraud or operational error: these zones simply do not receive yellow taxi service. This is a **data quality finding** (incorrect zone taxonomy), not an operational anomaly.

**Confidence:** HIGH — `dive_solid` shows 18-slice flat signal at theta, no variation. Structurally confirmed.

**Recommended action:** Exclude zones 103 and 110 from the zone taxonomy for yellow taxi analysis, or reclassify as "non-serviceable." Their permanent anomaly status pollutes the anomaly rate baseline.

### Finding 4: Ghost Trips — 114,783 Zero-Passenger Trips (CMT Vendor Dominant)

**Affected entities:** Vendor 1 (Creative Mobile Technologies), concentrated in zone 264 (N/A/Unknown)

**Detection path:** `aggregate(trip_pattern, group_by_line=vendors, event_filters={passenger_count<1})` revealed CMT vendor has **114,725** zero-passenger trips vs. VeriFone's 58.

**Root cause:**

| Vendor | Zero-Passenger Trips | Share |
|---|---|---|
| Creative Mobile Technologies (1) | 114,725 | 99.95% |
| VeriFone Inc (2) | 58 | 0.05% |

- Zone 264 ("N/A", Unknown borough) is the top pickup zone for zero-passenger trips: 15,185 trips — more than zones 161 (9,148), 236 (9,106), 237 (8,694).
- Rate code breakdown: Standard rate dominates zero-passenger trips (112,131 of 114,783), suggesting these are not airport dead-runs.
- These are almost certainly dead-miles or repositioning runs logged with passenger_count=0, or CMT meter-on-without-passenger events. The volume (114K trips = 1.5% of all CMT trips) is operationally significant.
- Cross-validation: zero-passenger trips spread across 248 zones — not localized to specific locations, consistent with repositioning rather than a single route exploit.

**False positive assessment:** Could be legitimate "no-passenger" states recorded during meter testing or GPS calibration. However, 114K events from one vendor vs. 58 from another strongly suggests a vendor-specific metering behavior difference, not a universal taxi operational pattern.

**Confidence:** HIGH — 2 independent aggregate queries confirm the 114K count and CMT dominance.

**Recommended action:** Exclude passenger_count=0 trips from revenue and performance metrics. Audit CMT metering logic for events that trigger trip logging without passenger confirmation. Compare CMT vs. VeriFone hardware/firmware event handling.

### Finding 5: Outside-NYC Destination Zone — Extreme Tip Pattern with Temporal Spike

**Affected entities:** Zone 265 ("Outside of NYC", N/A borough)

**Detection path:** `find_anomalies(zone_destination_pattern)` → zone 265 ranked #1 (delta_norm=8.07, 99.6th percentile). `explain_anomaly`: severity=high, driven by `_d_dropoff_avg_tip` (delta=7.23, 80.3% contribution). `dive_solid` revealed a single-week spike at 2019-01-15.

**Root cause:**
- Zone 265 has 13,897 dropoff trips, avg fare $70.77 (legitimate long-distance fare), avg distance 16.96 miles.
- `avg:tip_pct` for zone 265 destinations = **0.51 (51%)** — more than double the typical 20-23% for credit card trips.
- `dive_solid` snapshot at 2019-01-15: `_d_dropoff_avg_tip` delta=15.97 (delta_norm=16.36, massively anomalous), while all surrounding weeks are near normal (delta ~0.2-0.5). This is a **single-week spike** in tip percentage, not a sustained pattern.
- The zone_destination_pattern regime change (2019-08-27, magnitude 4.78) is driven by `_d_dropoff_avg_tip` shift (+3.93) — consistent with the tip metric being the primary instability signal for this pattern.
- Outside-NYC trips (likely JFK/EWR/LGA or Connecticut/NJ suburbs) appear structurally distinct: high avg fare, long distance, and the week of 2019-01-15 saw a small cohort of unusually large tips that overwhelmed the weekly avg.
- 4,484 credit card trips had tip_pct > 1 (>100% tip) vs. only 1 cash trip — confirming this is a credit card processing artifact or data entry error (digit transposition on tip entry).

**False positive assessment:** The 51% average tip for zone 265 dropoffs is inflated by the Jan 15 spike week (delta_norm=16.36). Outside that week, the zone is near-normal. The spike itself (likely ~50-200 trips with extreme tip_pct values) is almost certainly a data encoding issue — passengers tipping in dollar amounts being recorded as percentages or vice versa.

**Confidence:** HIGH — explain_anomaly, dive_solid, and aggregate avg:tip_pct all converge. Temporal isolation to single week is definitive.

**Recommended action:** Audit tip_pct records >1.0 for zone 265 dropoffs during week of 2019-01-14 to 2019-01-21. Implement tip_pct plausibility cap (e.g., >100% = data error) in ingestion. 4,484 credit card trips recorded tip_pct>1.0 across all zones — warrant systematic review.

### Finding 6: High-Speed Trips — Impossible Speed Records

**Affected entities:** 604 trips recorded at speed >80 mph; 3,667 trips at speed >50 mph

**Detection path:** `aggregate(trip_pattern, group_by_line=vendors, event_filters={speed_mph>80})` and speed >50 mph variants. Profiling alert on `speed_mph` (max=100.0, ratio=2.7).

**Root cause:**

| Vendor | Trips >50 mph | Avg Speed (>50mph cohort) | Trips >80 mph |
|---|---|---|---|
| Creative Mobile Technologies | 1,261 | 70.65 mph | 435 |
| VeriFone Inc | 2,406 | 57.23 mph | 169 |

- `speed_mph` is capped at 100.0 (the max value). NYC traffic rarely permits sustained highway speeds above 65 mph; 80+ mph is physically implausible for Manhattan trips.
- CMT trips >50 mph average 70.65 mph — suspiciously high. VeriFone trips average 57.23 mph.
- These likely represent GPS glitches (large coordinate jumps yielding artificially high speed calculations), or very short trips with near-zero duration.
- The profiling alert noted `speed_mph` max=100.0 with a capped ceiling — indicating the dataset may have pre-applied a 100 mph cap on an originally higher distribution.

**False positive assessment:** Some high-speed trips are legitimate (e.g., highway segments to JFK). The >80 mph cohort (604 trips, 0.008% of all trips) is most likely GPS artifact. The specific CMT dominance at extreme speeds correlates with the CMT zero-passenger anomaly (Finding 4), suggesting the CMT system has more GPS/metering artifacts overall.

**Confidence:** MEDIUM — aggregate confirms counts, mechanism (GPS error vs. legitimate highway) cannot be fully resolved without GPS traces.

**Recommended action:** Flag trips with computed speed >65 mph for GPS trace review. Investigate whether CMT devices have systematic GPS sampling issues causing speed outliers.

### Finding 7: Staten Island Zones — Low-Volume, High-Fare Structural Anomaly

**Affected entities:** Zone 44 (Charleston/Tottenville), Zone 5 (Arden Heights), Zones 84, 204 (Eltingville, Rossville/Woodrow)

**Detection path:** `find_anomalies(zone_activity_pattern)` → zone 44 delta_norm=5.65 (anomaly driven by fare_std, avg_fare, avg_distance). `find_anomalies(zone_destination_pattern)` → zones 44, 204, 84, 5 all appear with matching profile (avg_fare $74-85, avg_distance 25-32 miles, avg_duration 46-55 min).

**Root cause:**
- These are geographically remote Staten Island zones. Very few monthly trips (zone 44: 27 pickups; zone 5: 4 pickups) with high average fares ($54-74) and long distances (16-25 miles).
- The anomaly is structural — small sample size + genuinely unusual trip profile (long, expensive borough crossings) makes these zones geometric outliers.
- `passive_scan` confirmed both zone 44 and zone 5 appear anomalous in BOTH patterns (score=2, multi-source confirmed).
- Zone 44 tip_pct = 0.0 across all 27 trips — consistent with cash payment or no-charge, expected for long-distance Boro Zone trips.

**False positive assessment:** Partially structural. These zones are genuinely different from Manhattan Yellow Zone patterns (correct detection), but the "anomaly" is geographic/demographic rather than fraudulent or operational. The low trip counts make statistical inference unreliable.

**Confidence:** MEDIUM — dual-pattern confirmation (passive_scan score=2) supports finding; small sample limits root cause precision.

**Recommended action:** Treat Staten Island Boro Zones as a separate population segment for analytics. Anomalies within this segment should be benchmarked against other Boro Zone zones, not the full zone population.

### Finding 8: Temporal Zone Trajectory Anomalies — Arch/V-Shape Patterns Across Zone Population

**Affected entities:** 10 zones with arch trajectories, 10 with V-shape; top seeds: zone 92 (arch, wasted_motion=30.05), zone 194 (v_shape, 17.21), zone 265 (arch, 27.68)

**Detection path:** `detect_trajectory_anomaly(zone_activity_pattern)` and `detect_trajectory_anomaly(zone_destination_pattern)`. Both returned 10 results with displacement=0.0 and high wasted_motion across 18 temporal slices.

**Root cause:**
- All detected trajectory anomalies share `displacement=0.0` — the entity returned to exactly its starting position over the 18-week observation window.
- Temporal slice range: 2008-12-30 to 2088-01-20 — an 80-year window for a January 2019 dataset is anomalous in itself. The slice timestamps suggest the temporal index was built with a synthetic future-extended window rather than the actual observation period.
- The 18 slices map to roughly weekly snapshots during January 2019 (slices 521-556), then a single synthetic far-future slice (4125 at 2088-01-20).
- The arch/V-shape trajectories reflect real weekly seasonality within January 2019 (New Year's Day suppression → recovery → stabilization) — genuine signal.
- Zone 265 (Outside NYC) shows the most wasted motion in destination pattern (27.68) — consistent with Finding 5: the Jan 15 tip spike (delta_norm=16.36 for that week) creates an extreme arch followed by rapid return to baseline.

**False positive assessment:** The trajectory anomalies are REAL but the displacement=0.0 for all entities is partly a mathematical artifact of the long temporal window (displacement from 2008 baseline to 2088 endpoint, both of which are in the same "non-January" state). Within January 2019, the within-window motion is genuine weekly variation.

**Confidence:** MEDIUM — trajectory detection confirmed, but temporal window metadata (2008-2088) suggests calibration using a multi-year reference window, making direct displacement interpretation ambiguous.

**Recommended action:** Verify temporal index configuration. For January 2019 analysis, the effective observation window should be constrained to the month. Run `detect_trajectory_anomaly` on a sphere rebuilt with `timestamp_from=2019-01-01` and `timestamp_to=2019-01-31` to isolate intra-month arch trajectories.

### Finding 9: New Year's Day Effect — Volume Suppression and Pattern Shift

**Affected entities:** All 256 active pickup zones; top affected: zone 48 (Clinton East, 13,001 on Jan 1 vs. 12,048 on Jan 7), zone 79 (East Village), zone 186 (Penn Station area)

**Detection path:** `aggregate(trip_pattern, group_by_line=zones, time_from=2019-01-01, time_to=2019-01-02)` vs. `time_from=2019-01-07` (Monday reference). `compare_time_windows(zone_activity_pattern, Jan 1 vs Jan 7)`.

**Root cause:**
- Jan 1 total pickup activity across top-50 zones: 256 active zones, activity broadly distributed with late-night Manhattan party zones (Clinton East zone 48: 13K, East Village zone 79: 11.9K) leading — consistent with New Year's Eve celebration residual traffic.
- Jan 7 (first full Monday) shows different top zones: Upper East Side North (21.9K), Midtown Center (20.3K), Upper East Side South (20.2K) — office/commuter pattern.
- `compare_time_windows`: centroid shift = 0.234743 ("minor shift"). Top changing dimension: `_d_n_vendors` (diff=0.136) — more vendors active on weekday vs. holiday. The shift is real but modest in geometric terms.
- Regime change for zone_activity_pattern at **2019-02-19** (magnitude 4.63) with `_d_avg_tip_pct` shift +3.47 — this is NOT a New Year's effect but a later behavioral change (possibly related to congestion pricing discussions or payment behavior changes in February 2019).

**False positive assessment:** The New Year's Day spatial shift is a confirmed real-world behavioral pattern, not a data artifact. The geometric centroid shift is minor (0.23) because the overall zone population geometry is dominated by the Manhattan core which is active year-round.

**Confidence:** HIGH — two independent time-window comparisons confirm the shift. Minor magnitude expected for a one-day holiday effect.

**Recommended action:** Build a weekday/weekend/holiday-aware pattern for zone activity to avoid false positives from structural demand shifts. The February 2019 regime change deserves separate investigation (outside this dataset's scope).

### Finding 10: Cash Tip Zero-Inflation and Credit Card Tip Outliers

**Affected entities:** Payment type 2 (Cash), 2,090,269 trips; 4,484 credit card trips with tip_pct > 100%

**Detection path:** `aggregate(trip_pattern, group_by_line=payment_types, metric=avg:tip_amount)`. `aggregate(event_filters={tip_pct>1})` by payment type.

**Root cause:**
- Cash trips: avg tip_amount = **$0.00** (2,090,269 trips). Cash tips are systematically not recorded in TPEP — riders pay cash tips directly, bypassing the meter system. This creates an artificial zero-inflation in the tip dimension.
- Credit card trips: avg tip = $2.53. 4,484 credit card trips recorded tip_pct > 100% — impossible if the tip is applied to the fare. These are data entry errors or gratuity-as-fare encoding issues.
- The single cash trip with tip_pct > 1.0 (1 trip total) is likely a miscoded payment type.
- `zone_destination_pattern` regime change (2019-08-27) driven by `_d_dropoff_avg_tip` (+3.93): this confirms that tip behavior drifts over time, with the January 2019 window representing a distinct regime from later months in the temporal index.

**False positive assessment:** Cash tip zeros are NOT anomalies — they are a structural data collection limitation. The 4,484 extreme tip_pct records on credit cards ARE data quality issues (tip amounts entered in wrong field, currency confusion, or test transactions).

**Confidence:** HIGH — two aggregate queries converge. The structural zero-inflation is well-understood from TPEP documentation.

**Recommended action:** Exclude cash trips from any tip-rate analysis. Implement tip_pct > 1.0 flag for credit card records in data quality pipeline. Consider imputing cash tip rates from credit card behavior on matched trip profiles for comparative analysis.

## Methodology Notes

**Patterns checked:** All 3 patterns (trip_pattern, zone_activity_pattern, zone_destination_pattern). Total zone anomalies: 14 in each anchor pattern (5.28% rate).

**Scans executed:**
- Profiling alerts: all 5 dimensions in trip_pattern addressed
- Zone anomaly scan: both anchor patterns (find_anomalies, rank_by_property variants)
- Temporal: detect_trajectory_anomaly (both anchor patterns), find_drifting_entities (both), find_regime_changes (both), dive_solid (3 key entities), compare_time_windows
- Cross-pattern: passive_scan (threshold=1 and 2) on zones_pickup line
- Neighbor contamination: detect_neighbor_contamination on zone_activity_pattern (no contamination found — zones are not geometrically contaminating each other)
- Aggregate scans: 20+ aggregate calls covering payment types, rate codes, vendors, zones by event filters

**Coverage gaps:**
- `find_anomalies(trip_pattern)` failed with a datetime serialization error across all attempts — trip-level geometric anomalies could not be directly ranked. Mitigated by aggregate-based analysis and profiling alerts.
- Temporal window for zone patterns spans 2008-2088 (80 years) — this is a calibration artifact and does not reflect actual data range. All meaningful slices are within 2018-2019.
- No ground truth labels available (no fraud_flag, no known-bad-trip list) — precision cannot be computed.

**Sampling decisions:** No sampling applied — all zone-level patterns have 265 entities (full population); trip-level aggregates used full 7.56M trip dataset.
